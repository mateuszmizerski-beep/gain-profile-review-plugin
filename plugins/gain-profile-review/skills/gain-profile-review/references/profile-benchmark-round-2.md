## Scope

This second exercise reviewed 103 additional, deduplicated Full profiles headquartered in Poland. None of these profiles appeared in the original 54-profile cohort. The combined evidence base now covers 157 Polish Full profiles.

The goal remains to learn real platform wording and reviewer conventions without treating existing profiles as error-free ground truth.

## Refined findings

### 1. The three-block structure is a practical requirement

Across 157 profiles, 155 use exactly three prose blocks:

1. At a glance.
2. Customer base / Further information.
3. Market / Competitive position.

The two exceptions, Lancerto and Future Processing, contain only At a glance and customer paragraphs. Their absent Market blocks appear to be completeness issues rather than an alternative format.

Reviewer implication: treat a missing Market block as a likely material omission, not a stylistic variation. Reviewers should normally research and write the missing paragraph themselves because profiles are very rarely returned in practice. Leave it blank only when there is genuinely nothing defensible or useful to say.

### 2. At-a-glance length stabilized below the first cohort

The expanded median is 158 words and nine sentences, with an interquartile range of approximately eight to eleven sentences.

This reinforces the expert calibration:

- Six to eight sentences is soft guidance.
- Longer text is acceptable when it contains useful information.
- Never add content to reach a target.
- Review sequence, value, duplication, and sentence flow rather than applying a numeric cutoff.

### 3. Customer-first Further information is universal

Every one of the 157 profiles identifies B2B, B2C, B2G, and/or B2B2C exposure in the second block.

This is now sufficiently stable to treat missing customer exposure as a material Full-profile completeness issue.

### 4. Standard At-a-glance formulas remain dominant

Across the combined sample:

- `Business model revolves around` appears in 145 profiles.
- `Herein` appears in 76.
- `Additionally` appears in 51.
- `Furthermore` appears in 57.
- All 157 include headquarters and employee/FTE wording.

The platform deliberately favors standardized language. A reviewer should improve awkward repetition but should not rewrite conventional phrases merely to create stylistic novelty.

### 5. Differentiation is more common among mid-market profiles

Market differentiation appears in 83 of the 103 new profiles and 120 of 157 overall. Phrasing such as `aims`, `attempts`, `seeks`, or `tries to differentiate` appears in 101 combined profiles.

This is especially common where external market-share evidence is weak. The expert calibration remains appropriate:

- Search for more specific evidence first.
- Improve broad offering, geography, integration, or distribution statements by explaining the competitive consequence.
- Do not automatically delete a modestly generic statement when public information is genuinely limited.
- Avoid language so vague that it could describe almost any peer.

### 6. Self-claimed leadership is a stable platform convention

Thirty-nine of 157 Market blocks use explicit `claims` wording. The rate is almost identical between cohorts.

Reviewer implication:

- `Claims to be a leader` is acceptable when the statement comes from company materials.
- Preserve the attribution.
- Do not silently convert it into an objective fact.
- External corroboration adds value but is not required merely to retain the attributed claim.

### 7. Background structure remains highly stable

One hundred fifty-five of 157 profiles include a dated `As of` current-status sentence. The two exceptions were Euvic, which lacked an ownership-history text in the returned data, and EMC Instytut Medyczny, whose history ended with a 2019 acquisition without an explicit current-status sentence.

These appear to be review issues, not accepted variants.

Reviewer implication:

- Require a dated present ownership/listing sentence.
- Include a known majority or controlling holder, including for listed companies.
- For subsidiary assets, ownership wording depends on the structure. For PE- or VC-backed assets, name the financial sponsor investors rather than tracing every holding entity. Where a private individual's holding company is relevant to understanding control, the holding company and/or individual may be named.
- Do not accept an old transaction as a substitute for current ownership confirmation.

### 8. P&C count exceptions are symmetric

Combined configurations:

| Configuration | Profiles |
|---|---:|
| 3 Pros / 3 Cons | 138 |
| 3 Pros / 2 Cons | 6 |
| 2 Pros / 3 Cons | 6 |
| 2 Pros / 2 Cons | 7 |

The second cohort confirms that either side may contain only two arguments. Both first and final reviewers may approve this when a third point cannot be made client-ready.

### 9. P&C length is one of the strongest conventions

Across 915 non-empty arguments:

- Median: 99 characters.
- 75th percentile: 103.
- 91% fall within 90-120 characters.
- Only one argument exceeded 120 characters materially, at 124.
- One profile contained a blank third Con, which should be treated as a quality error rather than a two-Con presentation.

The practical rule remains:

- Target 90-103 characters.
- Accept up to approximately 120 where needed.
- Use abbreviations only when space requires them.
- Prefer a clean two-argument section to a blank or weak third argument.

### 10. Quantification is uneven between Pros and Cons

Across the combined sample:

- 52.8% of Pros contain a visible metric, ranking, percentage, or monetary datapoint.
- 16.2% of Cons contain comparable quantification.

This partly reflects the nature of risks, but also suggests an opportunity for reviewers to strengthen downside arguments with evidence such as:

- Customer or geographic concentration.
- Margin deterioration.
- CAPEX as a percentage of sales.
- Raw-material exposure.
- Regulatory incidents.
- Market-share loss.
- Revenue dependence on a product or customer.

Do not force a number where none exists, but check whether a generic risk can be grounded in available data.

### 11. Existing profiles still contain reviewable errors

The expanded sample includes examples of:

- Missing Market paragraphs.
- Missing or outdated current-ownership endings.
- A blank third Con.
- A 124-character Con.
- Grammar and typographical errors.
- Generic differentiation with weak economic implications.
- A Market block consisting almost entirely of an incomplete differentiation fragment.

Platform examples should teach structure and tone, not override reviewer judgment.

## Changes made to the model context

The combined evidence supports adding or strengthening these rules:

- Treat the three-block structure as a strong Full-profile completeness requirement.
- Treat customer exposure in Further information as mandatory.
- Treat a missing Market block as a material omission that the reviewer normally fills directly; leave blank only when no useful, defensible content can be found.
- Treat a missing dated current-ownership sentence as a material omission.
- Prefer two P&Cs over a blank or non-client-ready third point.
- Consider quantifying Cons where evidence is reasonably available.
- Preserve conventional Gain wording while improving repetition, grammar, and logic.
- Continue using peer profiles as format evidence rather than factual or analytical authority.

## Expert reviewer calibration

1. **Missing Market paragraph:** Reviewers should normally research and write it themselves. Profiles are very rarely returned in practice. If there is truly nothing useful or defensible to say, the Market field may remain blank.
2. **Minimum Market content:** A sourced size classification plus a concise description of the competitive set is sufficient when reliable rankings and compelling USPs are unavailable.
3. **Source recency:** Market-share, ranking, and market-size evidence should generally be no more than four years old. Older datapoints should not be used as current evidence merely because they are dated; retain them only as explicitly historical context when the comparison itself is relevant.
4. **Quantifying Cons:** Quantify downside arguments when applicable and useful, but never force a datapoint.
5. **Forward-looking P&Cs:** `Set to benefit`, `well-positioned`, `likely to`, and similar formulations may rest on sound analyst judgment supported by business and market logic. A specific external forecast is not mandatory.
6. **Subsidiary profiles:** Apply case judgment. For PE/VC ownership, name the investors rather than intermediate holding companies. Where a private individual's holding company is relevant to control, naming the holding company and/or individual is appropriate.
