# Gain.pro Poland Full Profiles: Practical Wording Benchmark

Version: 2026-06-12

## Purpose

This report compares the written Gain.pro profiling guidance with wording observed in 54 live Full profiles headquartered in Poland. The objective is to teach a reviewing model how the platform's theory is applied in practice across sectors and ownership structures.

This is an empirical benchmark, not an automatic replacement for current Notion guidance. Differences between live-profile practice and written policy are interpreted using the expert-reviewer calibration recorded below.

## Main findings

### 1. Every profile uses the same three-block Business architecture

All 54 business descriptions contain exactly three paragraphs:

1. At a glance / core business description.
2. Customer base and further information.
3. Competitive positioning / Market.

This is the strongest observed structural convention. A reviewer should treat missing, merged, or misordered blocks as a likely consistency issue unless the profile type or CMS field structure explains it.

### 2. At a glance is more formulaic and longer than the theoretical target suggests

Observed At-a-glance statistics:

- Median length: 164 words.
- Interquartile range: 146-178 words.
- Median sentence count: approximately 10.
- Interquartile range: approximately 9-11 sentences.

Common sequence:

1. `[Asset] is a [identity].`
2. `The Group comprises...` where applicable.
3. `[Asset]'s business model revolves around...`
4. `Herein...` to enumerate the core offering.
5. `Additionally...` or `Furthermore...` for adjacent offerings.
6. Revenue-generation sentence.
7. Dated operating KPI sentence.
8. Headquarters and employee sentence.

Frequency within the 54 profiles:

- Identity phrasing: 46.
- Group-composition sentence: 30.
- `Business model revolves around`: 49.
- `Herein`: 24.
- `Additionally`: 15.
- `Furthermore`: 23.
- Explicit revenue-generation wording: 42 under a strict formula test and 49 under a broader revenue/sales test.
- Dated `As of` KPI wording: 50.
- Headquarters wording: 54.
- Employee/FTE/people wording: 53.

Practical implication: the 6-8 sentence direction is soft guidance. Reviewers should focus on completeness, redundancy, and readability rather than rejecting a longer paragraph. More sentences are acceptable when there is genuinely more useful information; never add content merely to reach a target.

### 3. Business wording is deliberately standardized

Recurring wording is a platform convention rather than weak writing by itself:

- `The Company's business model revolves around...`
- `Herein, the Company offers...`
- `Additionally...`
- `Furthermore...`
- `The Company generates transactional/contractual revenue...`
- `As of [date]...`
- `[Asset] is headquartered in [city] (PL) and had [N] employees...`

However, repeated transition words can still create mechanical prose. Review for flow and unnecessary duplication, not for originality.

### 4. Further information strongly confirms the customer-first rule

All 54 second paragraphs explicitly identify customer type. Forty-four use the verb `addresses`; the remainder mostly use `serves`, `caters to`, or equivalent wording.

Common opening:

`[Asset] primarily addresses a B2B/B2C/B2G customer base comprising...`

Observed additions include:

- Customer sectors and end markets.
- Named clients.
- Geographic customer reach.
- Distribution channels.
- Outsourcing or sourcing details.
- Facilities and operational facts.
- Partnerships, although these appeared relatively infrequently.

Practical implication: a Full profile whose Further information section does not open with customer exposure is outside the dominant live-platform convention.

### 5. Market sections prioritize position, but generic differentiation remains common

Observed Market frequencies:

- Position, ranking, market share, leadership, or player-size wording: 50 of 54.
- Differentiation wording: 37 of 54.
- `Attempts`, `aims`, `seeks`, or `tries` to differentiate: 28 of 54.
- Company claims explicitly qualified with `claims`: 14 of 54.
- Market-size or growth context: 18 of 54.
- Visible external source/date attribution: 32 of 54.

Typical Market opening types:

- Objective ranking: `[Asset] ranked #X...`
- Qualified company claim: `[Asset] claims to be...`
- Analyst size assessment: `[Asset] is a medium/large-sized player...`
- Externally framed position: `[Asset] is considered to be...`

Typical closing:

`The Company aims/attempts to differentiate itself through...`

The live sample confirms that market position is the anchor. It also shows frequent use of generic differentiators such as broad offering, geographic reach, vertical integration, or distribution networks. Reviewers should preferably improve these points through further research and make them more specific. Do not delete them automatically: some companies have limited public differentiation and a defensible, modestly generic point may be the best available conclusion. Avoid vague wording wherever stronger evidence can reasonably be found.

### 6. Background wording is highly consistent

Observed Background frequencies:

- Founding/establishment/history origin near the opening: 52 of 54.
- Rebranding mentioned where relevant: 15.
- Acquisition, sale, IPO, listing, merger, or stake event: 37.
- Dated current ownership introduced with `As of`: 54.

Dominant structure:

1. Origin, founding year, and founders.
2. Selected ownership or transformation events.
3. Dated present ownership/listing sentence.

Private and sponsor-owned profiles normally name the current owners clearly. Examples of observed structures include:

- `...majority-owned by [owner], with [investors] holding minority stakes.`
- `...solely-owned by [holding company/person].`
- `...majority-owned by its founders, with minority stakes held by...`

Listed profiles are less uniform. Some name a controlling founder alongside the listing; others end only with `remained listed on the WSE`. When a person or investor holds a known majority or controlling stake, include that information. A simple listing statement can be sufficient when no controlling shareholder exists or is identified.

### 7. P&C counts validate the reviewer exception

Observed configurations:

| Configuration | Profiles |
|---|---:|
| 3 Pros / 3 Cons | 48 |
| 3 Pros / 2 Cons | 3 |
| 2 Pros / 2 Cons | 3 |

The live platform confirms that three per side is the standard and two is a real but uncommon reviewer exception.

### 8. The tight P&C character target is strongly supported

Across 315 observed arguments:

- Minimum: 72 characters.
- 25th percentile: 95.
- Median: 100.
- 75th percentile: 103.
- Maximum: 117.
- 207 arguments were within 90-103 characters.
- 286 arguments were within 90-120 characters.

This supports using 90-103 as the practical drafting/review target and 90-120 as the acceptable outer range.

### 9. P&Cs use recognizable argument families

Common Pro themes:

- Growth: 48 profiles.
- Leadership/position: 43.
- Diversification or broad portfolio: 37.
- International presence: 31.
- Market tailwinds/demand: 20.
- Margin/profitability: 17.
- Revenue visibility/recurrence: 10.

Common Con themes:

- Competition: 41 profiles.
- CAPEX, asset intensity, or cash generation: 28.
- Concentration/dependency/exposure: 27.
- Input costs, wages, energy, or inflation: 26.
- Margin/profitability pressure: 21.
- Regulation/legal risk: 18.
- Cyclicality/downturn exposure: 13.

Numeric support appeared in 86 Pros and 25 Cons. Live profiles therefore quantify positives substantially more often than risks.

### 10. Practical P&C grammar

Observed construction generally follows:

`Characteristic + supporting fact/metric + implication.`

Common conventions:

- No final full stop.
- `+X% CAGR` and `+Xpp EBITDA` notation.
- Parenthetical periods and examples.
- Abbreviations such as `LT`, `int'l`, `w/`, `avg`, `B2B/C/G`, and `e.g.`.
- `#1`, `#2`, or `top-3` ranking language.
- Semicolons or participial phrases to add the implication within the character limit.

The strongest arguments connect evidence and consequence. Weak live examples still occur, particularly generic competition, broad-offering, labor-shortage, or regulation statements without company-specific proof.

## Theory versus practice

| Topic | Written guidance | Live-profile evidence | Recommended model behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| Business structure | Prescribed Business components | Exactly three paragraphs in all 54 | Treat three-block structure as a strong Full-profile convention |
| At-a-glance length | Approximately 6-8 sentences | Median approximately 10 | Treat as soft guidance; allow more when useful and never force extra content |
| Revenue generation | Expected in standard sequence, reviewer discretion for obvious models | Present in nearly all profiles | Require economic model clarity; allow integrated rather than standalone wording |
| Further information | Customer-first | Customer type present in all 54 | Treat missing customer exposure as a material completeness issue |
| Market differentiation | Not required when position is clear; avoid weak USPs | Differentiation appears in 37 | Research and sharpen vague points; retain modest generic points when no stronger public differentiation exists |
| Market size | Optional/non-core | Appears in 18 | Include only when reliable and useful for contextualizing position or growth |
| Background ending | Name current owner(s) and date | All dated; listed-company detail varies | Include any known majority/controlling shareholder, including for listed assets |
| P&C count | Three per side, reviewer can retain two | Six profiles use fewer than 3/3 | Preserve as rare quality exception |
| P&C length | 90-120; review target 90-103 | Median 100, p75 103 | Use 90-103 as target and 120 as hard practical ceiling |
| Recent versus long-term ratings | Five-year framework with recent weighting | P&Cs often use recent 4-5 year windows | Continue rating framework; explicitly challenge recent divergence |

## Quality issues visible even in live profiles

Existing platform profiles are useful style evidence, not error-free ground truth. The sample contains examples of:

- Duplicate customer types, such as `B2G and B2G` where another category may have been intended.
- Generic differentiation based on product breadth or geography.
- Awkward causal links in P&Cs.
- Grammar issues caused by compression into character limits.
- Company claims retained without strong independent corroboration.
- Inconsistent detail for current shareholders of listed companies.

A model should learn the platform's architecture and tone without imitating its mistakes.

## Expert reviewer calibration

1. **At-a-glance length:** The 6-8 sentence rule is not hard. Longer text is acceptable when there is more relevant information. Do not force additional content.
2. **Listed-company ownership:** Include a majority or controlling shareholder whenever one exists and is known. A listing-only ending is acceptable when no controller is identified.
3. **Generic differentiation:** Prefer additional research and sharpen vague points. Do not delete them automatically when the company genuinely offers little public evidence of distinctive positioning, but avoid super-vague wording where possible.
4. **Company leadership claims:** `Claims to be a leader` is the normal formulation when the leadership claim comes from the company's own website or materials. It communicates provenance rather than independent verification.
5. **P&C abbreviations:** Use abbreviations such as `LT`, `int'l`, `w/`, and `B2B/C/G` when space requires them, not as the default when the full wording fits.
6. **Two P&Cs:** Both first and final reviewers may approve two Pros and/or Cons when a third cannot be made client-ready. It remains an exception rather than the standard.

## Practical review heuristics added from the sample

- Expect exactly three Business/Market prose blocks in a Polish Full profile.
- Check the At-a-glance sequence, not just sentence count.
- Require customer exposure at the beginning of Further information.
- Treat Market position/ranking as the anchor and differentiation as secondary.
- Attribute company-sourced leadership claims explicitly using `claims to be` or equivalent wording.
- Require every Background to end with a dated current-status sentence.
- Use 90-103 characters as the P&C target.
- Do not copy generic live-profile P&Cs without validating company applicability.
- Compare P&C claims with Business, Market, Financials, and ratings.
- Use P&C abbreviations only when needed for space.
- First and final reviewers may approve two P&Cs as a quality exception.
- Learn format from existing profiles, but continue to review their logic and accuracy independently.
