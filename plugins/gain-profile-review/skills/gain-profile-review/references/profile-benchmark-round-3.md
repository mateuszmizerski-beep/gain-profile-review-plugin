## Scope

This round reviewed 75 additional, deduplicated Full profiles headquartered in Poland. None appeared in the first 157-profile evidence base. The combined benchmark now covers 232 Polish Full profiles.

Unlike the earlier broad samples, this cohort was deliberately weighted toward underrepresented and difficult review situations: financial services, payments, sponsor-backed assets, minority investments, subsidiaries, software and technical services, healthcare, environmental services, and private consumer businesses with limited public disclosure.

Existing profiles are used as evidence of platform structure and tone, not as error-free analytical authority.

## Findings and refinements

### 1. Structure remains more stable than wording

All 75 profiles contain the expected three blocks:

1. At a glance.
2. Customer base / Further information.
3. Market / Competitive position.

All also contain a dated current-status sentence in Background. This makes the two missing Market blocks and two incomplete Backgrounds found in the earlier sample look even more clearly like review defects rather than accepted variants.

### 2. At a glance should expand with business complexity, not to meet a quota

The third-cohort median is 149 words and nine sentences. Financial groups, diversified industrial groups, and multi-product software businesses often need more space to explain the offering and revenue model. Simpler private businesses are usually shorter.

Review the sequence and informational density. Do not force six to eight sentences, and do not shorten a useful explanation merely because it exceeds a soft benchmark.

### 3. Sector-aware Business wording improves accuracy

The cohort adds several useful patterns:

- **Banks and fintech:** group products into understandable families, identify interest, commission, transaction, subscription, or service-fee revenue, and distinguish B2C, B2B, and B2G exposure.
- **Software and technical services:** explain whether revenue is subscription, licence, implementation, project, maintenance, managed-service, or usage based. Do not describe every technology without explaining what the customer buys.
- **Consumer and food:** distinguish branded, private-label, distribution, retail, and manufacturing activities. State whether production is in-house or outsourced when material.
- **Environmental and infrastructure services:** explain the physical asset base, contract model, capacity, and regulatory exposure where relevant.
- **Healthcare:** distinguish facility ownership, treatment delivery, diagnostics, veterinary services, and network aggregation.

These are analytical distinctions, not mandatory templates.

### 4. Market should use the strongest available evidence, not a rigid three-item formula

Within the third cohort:

- 54 of 75 contain position, ranking, leadership, or market-share wording.
- 62 contain differentiation wording.
- 44 quantify market size.
- 28 explicitly attribute a claim to the company.

Many strong Market paragraphs do not contain all three of market size, ranking, and differentiation. Strong, recent market-share or ranking evidence may carry the paragraph without a generic USP. Conversely, where ranking evidence is unavailable, a sourced size classification, credible competitive set, and economically meaningful differentiation can be sufficient.

Reviewer implication: require a coherent view of market position, but choose the evidence mix according to what is actually knowable.

### 5. The four-year rule must be applied to the datapoint, not merely the source date

The cohort contains live examples relying on 2020 or 2021 market-share/ranking evidence, including JNT Group, Grupa Ekoenergetyka, and Ecowipes. These should not be repeated as current position evidence in a 2026 review.

Use market-share, ranking, and market-size evidence no more than approximately four years old. Older evidence may be mentioned only as explicitly historical context when the historical change itself is relevant; dating an old datapoint does not make it current evidence.

Forecast horizons and historical strategy dates are different. For example, a strategy adopted in 2017 with a 2030 target is not itself a stale current market-share datapoint.

### 6. `Claims` is useful provenance, not a substitute for judgment

Explicit company-claim wording is more common in this cohort because private and sponsor-backed businesses often lack independent rankings. Preserve `claims to be` when the source is the company itself.

Still test whether the claimed market is sensibly defined. A narrow or invented category should not be presented uncritically merely because attribution has been added.

### 7. Backgrounds for sponsor-backed assets follow a stable compression pattern

Strong examples generally contain:

1. Founding year and founder or underlying-business origin.
2. Only the ownership transactions, carve-outs, mergers, or rebrandings needed to understand the present asset.
3. A dated sentence naming the current sponsor/controller and material minority holders.

Live examples consistently name sponsors such as Mid Europa Partners, Enterprise Investors, Abris Capital Partners, Innova Capital, Accel-KKR, Oaktree, and Cornerstone rather than tracing every intermediate legal vehicle.

Two profiles end with ownership confirmations from 2024. This is not itself a review concern: use the latest reliable ownership information available without applying a separate freshness threshold.

### 8. Two-point Assessment sections are a genuine quality exception

Third-cohort configurations:

| Configuration | Profiles |
|---|---:|
| 3 Pros / 3 Cons | 63 |
| 3 Pros / 2 Cons | 6 |
| 2 Pros / 3 Cons | 2 |
| 2 Pros / 2 Cons | 4 |

Examples with two points include e-point, Hortex, Grupa Pracuj, Beyond.pl, Standard, JNT Group, Grupa Ekoenergetyka, Endego, Welyo, Morele, and Sfinks Polska. No argument was blank.

This supports approving two strong points where the third would be repetitive, vague, or immaterial. It does not support treating two as the new default.

### 9. Assessment length is stable, but compression can damage quality

Across 434 third-cohort arguments:

- Median length is 98 characters.
- The 75th percentile is 103 characters.
- 93% fall within 90-120 characters.
- None exceed 120 characters.

The cohort also contains awkward abbreviations and compressed phrases such as unexplained `BM`, unnecessary `w/`, excessive ampersands, and period ranges shortened to `18-22`. These are precedent for the character constraint, not preferred writing. Use abbreviations only when clarity would otherwise survive and space genuinely requires them.

### 10. Quantification remains much more natural in Pros than Cons

Approximately 58% of third-cohort Pros and 19% of Cons contain a visible numeric datapoint under a broad count. The exact percentage depends on whether dates and non-performance numbers are treated as quantification, but the directional result is stable.

Reviewers should look for useful downside evidence such as margin contraction, geographic/customer concentration, CAPEX intensity, revenue decline, or product dependence. Do not insert a weak number merely to make a Con appear analytical.

### 11. Practical error patterns to teach the model

The third cohort contains examples of:

- A reversed CAGR period (`2025-2020`).
- Market-share or ranking evidence older than the expert-approved threshold.
- Strong company claims attached to overly narrow market definitions.
- Missing employee/FTE wording where public data may be unavailable.
- Generic differentiation that states a feature without its competitive consequence.
- P&Cs compressed enough to become unnatural or ambiguous.
- Missing known retained founder stakes where they would usefully clarify a sponsor-backed ownership structure.

The model should flag these even when similar wording exists in published profiles.

## Combined operational conclusions

The 232-profile evidence base supports the following stable rules:

- Full profiles normally contain At a glance, customer/Further information, and Market blocks.
- Full-profile Further information always identifies customer exposure.
- At a glance normally ends with headquarters and a dated employee/FTE figure. If unavailable after reasonable research, omit it without a separate workflow flag.
- Background normally ends with a dated, currently verified ownership or listing statement.
- Market content should establish a defensible position using the strongest available combination of size, ranking/share, competitors, and differentiation.
- Company-sourced leadership claims should remain attributed.
- Current market-share, ranking, and market-size evidence should be no more than about four years old.
- Three Pros and three Cons remain the default; two on either side are acceptable when genuinely stronger than a weak third.
- P&Cs should generally remain within 90-120 characters, with 90-103 as the practical sweet spot.
- Quantification is valuable when it sharpens the investment point, especially for downside, but is not mandatory.
- Live profiles establish Gain tone and structure; they do not override factual, grammatical, or analytical review judgment.

## Expert reviewer calibration

1. **Unavailable employee/FTE data:** Omit it after reasonable research. Do not invent it or add a separate workflow flag.
2. **Ownership-date freshness:** Do not apply a preferred freshness threshold during review; use the latest reliable information available.
3. **Retained founder stakes:** Include them when known and useful, but not as an absolute requirement in every PE/VC-backed profile.
4. **Market share without differentiation:** Strong, recent share or ranking evidence may stand without a separate differentiation statement.
5. **Market-size recency:** Apply the same four-year ceiling used for market share and rankings.
